Scientific background

Many astrophysical phenomena require multi-dimensional approaches. 2DStars is aimed to be a code facility that is able to model a variety of such phenomena encountered in the evolution of single and binary stars, like rotation, stellar formation, close binary systems, tidally-locked x-ray binaries etc...\\ The theory of stellar evolution has been largely successful, thus rotation was and still is generally overlooked. However, a number of serious discrepancies between current models and observations have been noticed recently. In particular, helium and nitrogen abundances in massive O- and B-type stars and in giants and supergiants, as well as the distribution of stars in the HR diagram at various metallicities (Maeder \& Meynet 2000). In fact, stellar models are greatly influenced by rotation by various degrees depending on the rotational velocity (tracks in the HR diagram, lifetimes, masses, surface abundances …). Stellar evolution is thus a function of M, Z and $\Omega$.

Stellar rotation

A large fraction of stars rotate rapidly. The centrifugal force caused by axial rotation disturbs the hydrostatic balance, which alters the structure. This affects intrinsic stellar properties like luminosity (Potter et al. 2012a), oscillation frequencies (Reese et al. 2014) etc.. Rotation not only causes aspherical distortion but also surface temperature variations, which introduces a brightness asymmetry due to the variation in the flux flowing through the surface as a function of latitude. This distinctive observational feature was first described by von Zeipel’s theorem (Von Zeipel 1924), which predicted that the emergent radiative flux at any point over the surface of a rotating star in radiative equilibrium varies proportionally to the local gravity g. In other words, more flux flows out at higher latitudes, which is also known as \textit{"gravity darkening"}. \\ Fig. \ref{fig1} shows an image of \textit{Altair}, which rotates at 90\% of its breakup velocity with a period of 9 hours (2.8 rev/day). This causes the equator to bulge and darken (become cooler). The intensity of the equator is about 60\% that at the pole.
